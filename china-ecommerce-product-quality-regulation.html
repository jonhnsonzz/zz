China's E-Commerce Product Regulations: Platform Liability and Quality Compliance

How the E-Commerce Law and Product Quality Law create joint liability for platforms and sellers
June 4, 2026

China's e-commerce market surpassed RMB 13.8 trillion in gross merchandise volume in 2025. Foreign brands selling on any Chinese platform face a multi-layered regulatory framework that holds both sellers and platforms jointly liable for product quality failures. This article applies the 4-layer framework to understand these regulations and their practical implications.

Layer 0: Why China Regulates E-Commerce Product Quality

China's e-commerce product quality regime is driven by three interconnected policy objectives:

Consumer Protection

The 2024 consumer rights white paper recorded over 10 million e-commerce-related complaints, with product quality accounting for 38% of all disputes. Counterfeit and substandard goods remain the top consumer grievance category on Chinese platforms.

Platform Accountability

Unlike Western models where platforms are largely intermediaries shielded by safe harbors, China's legal framework imposes active monitoring and due diligence obligations on platform operators. The regulatory logic: platforms profit from transactions and should bear corresponding responsibility for the quality of goods flowing through their ecosystems.

Market Trust Repair

High-profile scandals — from counterfeit infant formula to fake luxury goods on livestreaming channels — have eroded consumer confidence. The State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) has made e-commerce product quality a priority enforcement area in its 2025–2027 work plan.

Value for decision-makers: China's approach is to regulate the platform, not just the seller. This means foreign brands cannot rely on platform warranties alone — they must build their own compliance infrastructure.

Layer 1: The Core Legal Framework

Four regulatory pillars govern product quality in Chinese e-commerce. Foreign brands face exposure under all of them.

1. E-Commerce Law Article 38 — Platform Due Diligence Obligation

Article 38 is the single most consequential provision. It states that where a platform "knows or should know" that a seller is infringing consumer rights and fails to act, it shall be jointly and severally liable with the seller. This creates dual liability: consumers can sue both seller and platform for damages, and SAMR can issue fines up to RMB 2 million for serious violations. The "should know" standard is broad — courts have interpreted it to require proactive monitoring for red flags.

2. Product Quality Law Article 33 — Seller Inspection Duty

Sellers must establish an incoming goods inspection system and verify quality certificates for every batch. Key implications: documentary proof must follow the goods through the supply chain; no "pass-through" defense — a foreign brand selling through a distributor remains liable; penalties include fines of 50% to 300% of the value of substandard goods, and criminal liability for repeat offenses.

3. AIGC Deep Synthesis Algorithm Filing

Platforms using AI for product authentication must file their algorithm with the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC), submit technical documentation, and undergo periodic security assessments. This includes image recognition systems for luxury goods authentication. Over 6,800 algorithms registered as of mid-2026.

4. Cross-Border E-Commerce Retail Import Rules

Foreign brands selling via CBEC channels face additional requirements: pre-market registration for first-import products (strictest for cosmetics, health foods), Chinese-language labeling with GB standard compliance marks, and domestic service agent appointment for returns and recalls. Single transaction limit: RMB 5,000; annual per-person cap: RMB 26,000.

Sources: E-Commerce Law (2019), Product Quality Law (2023 revision), CAC Algorithm Filing Provisions, Ministry of Commerce CBEC Notice (2018 revision). All publicly available.

Layer 2: How Enforcement Works in Practice

Nationwide Product Quality Spot-Checks

SAMR conducts quarterly random sampling of e-commerce products. In 2025, the spot-check covered 42 product categories testing 68 quality indicators. Products that fail are publicly listed with seller name, platform, and specific violation — causing immediate reputational damage.

Graduated Penalty Structure

First offenses typically receive warnings and corrective orders. Repeat or serious violations incur fines (RMB 50,000–2 million), product confiscation, and business suspension. For cases involving bodily injury or value exceeding RMB 50,000, the case is referred for criminal prosecution under Article 140 of the Criminal Law.

Platform-Driven Enforcement

Increasingly, platforms act as enforcement agents to avoid their own Article 38 liability. JD.com, Alibaba, and Pinduoduo operate dedicated quality assurance teams that conduct random seller sampling. A 2%+ complaint rate on product quality typically results in delisting and blacklisting.

During SAMR's 2024 nationwide spot-check, approximately 50% of fashion and luxury goods samples from one major authentication platform failed verification against listed specifications — illustrating the scale of quality gaps regulators are addressing.

Key consideration: Enforcement in China is increasingly data-driven. SAMR uses complaint data, spot-check results, and platform-reported metrics to target enforcement. Sellers with a higher-than-average complaint ratio attract disproportionate regulatory attention.

Layer 3: Practical Paths Forward

1. Document Every Link in the Supply Chain

Maintain quality certificates, factory inspection reports, and customs clearance documents for every batch. Digitize and archive with a minimum 3-year retention period.

2. Invest in Chinese-Language Labeling Compliance

This is the single most common enforcement action against foreign brands. Work with a local labeling specialist to ensure every SKU meets GB standard marking requirements before listing.

3. Verify Platform Algorithm Filings

If the platform uses AI for quality authentication, confirm the algorithm is filed with CAC. Request filing confirmation as part of platform onboarding.

4. Appoint a Domestic Service Agent

For CBEC channels, ensure a registered Chinese entity is contractually obligated to handle returns, refunds, recalls, and regulatory inquiries.

Note: The compliance burden varies significantly by product category and sales channel. A luxury handbag seller faces different requirements than an electronics brand. Customized analysis based on specific products and market entry strategy is recommended.
