China's E-Commerce Product Quality Regulations: What Foreign Brands Need to Know

How the E-Commerce Law, Product Quality Law, and cross-border rules create a layered compliance burden for online sellers — and why getting it wrong is expensive.

China Regulatory Research Analyst • June 2026

China's e-commerce market surpassed RMB 13.8 trillion in gross merchandise volume in 2025[1]. With that scale comes aggressive regulatory enforcement. Foreign brands selling on Taobao, JD, Pinduoduo, Douyin, or any Chinese platform face a multi-layered regulatory framework that holds both sellers and platforms jointly liable for product quality failures. This article breaks down the laws that matter, how enforcement works in practice, and what compliance steps foreign sellers should take now.

Why China Regulates E-Commerce Product Quality

China's e-commerce product quality regime is driven by three interconnected policy objectives:

  • Consumer protection: The 2024 consumer rights white paper recorded over 10 million e-commerce-related complaints, with product quality accounting for 38% of all disputes[2]. Counterfeit and substandard goods remain the top consumer grievance category on Chinese platforms.
  • Platform accountability: Unlike Western models where platforms are largely intermediaries shielded by Section 230-type safe harbors, China's legal framework imposes active monitoring and due diligence obligations on platform operators. The regulator's logic: platforms profit from transactions and should bear corresponding responsibility for the quality of goods flowing through their ecosystems.
  • Market trust repair: High-profile scandals — from counterfeit infant formula to fake luxury goods on livestreaming channels — have eroded consumer confidence. The State Administration for Market Regulation (SAMR) has made e-commerce product quality a "priority enforcement area" in its 2025–2027 work plan[3].

The result is a regulatory system that combines statutory liability for platforms, administrative penalties for sellers, and escalating enforcement through nationwide product quality spot-checks.

The Core Legal Framework

Four regulatory pillars govern product quality in Chinese e-commerce. Foreign brands face exposure under all of them.

1. E-Commerce Law Article 38 — Platform Due Diligence Obligation

Article 38 of the E-Commerce Law of the People's Republic of China (2019) is the single most consequential provision for platform operators. It states that:

"Where an e-commerce platform operator knows or should know that a seller on its platform is infringing upon the legitimate rights and interests of consumers, and fails to take necessary measures, it shall be jointly and severally liable with the seller."

— E-Commerce Law, Article 38, Paragraph 2[4]

This creates a dual liability structure:

  • Civil liability: Consumers can sue both the seller and the platform for full damages, including punitive damages for counterfeit goods. Platforms that fail to verify seller qualifications or monitor product quality face unlimited joint liability.
  • Administrative liability: SAMR can issue warnings, fines (up to RMB 2 million for serious violations), order business suspension for rectification, and even revoke platform operating permits[5].

The "should know" standard is broad. Chinese courts have interpreted it to mean that platforms must proactively monitor for red flags — unusually low pricing, high complaint ratios, suspicious product descriptions — rather than passively waiting for consumer complaints. In practice, this forces platforms to invest heavily in quality control infrastructure.

For context: during SAMR's 2024 nationwide product quality spot-check, approximately 50% of the fashion and luxury goods samples from one major online marketplace failed verification against merchant-listed specifications[6]. While this case involves a specific platform (DeWu), it illustrates the scale of quality gaps that regulators and platforms alike are now addressing.

2. Product Quality Law Article 33 — Seller Inspection Duty

The Product Quality Law (revised 2023) imposes direct obligations on every seller in the distribution chain. Article 33 requires that:

"A seller shall establish and implement an incoming goods inspection and acceptance system, and verify the quality certificates and other identifying marks of the products it sells."

— Product Quality Law, Article 33[7]

Key implications for foreign sellers:

  • Documentary proof must follow the goods. Quality certificates, factory inspection reports, and import customs clearance documents must be maintained for each product batch. Failure to produce these upon SAMR inspection creates a presumption of non-compliance.
  • No "pass-through" defense. A foreign brand that sells through a Chinese distributor and claims ignorance of substandard fulfillment will still be held liable as the ultimate seller. The law follows the chain of commerce, not the chain of contracts.
  • Penalties are severe. Selling substandard products can result in confiscation of illegal income, fines of 50% to 300% of the value of the substandard goods, and criminal liability for repeat or egregious offenses[8].

3. AIGC Deep Synthesis Algorithm Filing

A newer regulatory layer involves the Administrative Provisions on Deep Synthesis in Internet-Based Information Services (effective 2023). While this might seem unrelated to product quality at first glance, it has direct relevance for platforms using AI-powered authentication and quality assessment tools.

Platforms that deploy algorithms to verify product authenticity — such as AI-based image recognition systems for luxury goods authentication — are required to:

  • File their algorithm with the Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC) through the official algorithm filing system
  • Submit technical documentation explaining how the algorithm works, its accuracy rates, and its bias mitigation measures
  • Undergo periodic security assessments

Why this matters for product quality: If a platform uses AI to verify product authenticity and that system fails (or is not properly filed), both the platform and the seller face regulatory exposure. The first generation of such filings — including Dewu's AI sneaker identification algorithm, which was among the earliest to be registered in the CAC's algorithm filing inventory[9] — set a precedent that quality-adjacent algorithms are subject to regulatory scrutiny.

Foreign brands selling authenticated goods should confirm whether the platform's quality verification algorithms (image recognition, barcode verification, RFID authentication) are properly filed. An unfiled algorithm could be grounds for regulators to question the platform's entire quality assurance process.

4. Cross-Border E-Commerce Retail Import Rules

For foreign brands selling into China via cross-border e-commerce (CBEC) channels — including Tmall Global, JD Worldwide, Kaola, and Douyin Global — additional product quality regulations apply under the Notice on Improving the Supervision of Cross-Border E-Commerce Retail Imports (Ministry of Commerce, 2018, as updated).

Key quality compliance requirements under the CBEC framework include:

  • Pre-market registration: Products in the "First import" category must undergo registration or filing with SAMR before being sold. Foreign cosmetics, health foods, and infant formula are subject to the strictest pre-market approval requirements[12].
  • Labeling compliance: All CBEC imports must carry Chinese-language labels with: product name, manufacturer name and address, production date, shelf life, and Chinese safety standards compliance marks (e.g., GB standards). Failure to label correctly is the single most common SAMR enforcement action against foreign brands[13].
  • After-sales service obligation: CBEC platforms are required to maintain a domestic entity responsible for returns, refunds, and recalls. Foreign brands without a China-based service entity must appoint a domestic agent.[14]

How Enforcement Works

Understanding the letter of the law is only half the picture. Enforcement patterns reveal where the real risk lies for foreign brands.

Nationwide Product Quality Spot-Checks

SAMR conducts quarterly random sampling of e-commerce products across major platforms. In 2025, SAMR's e-commerce-specific spot-check covered 42 product categories, testing for 68 quality indicators including safety, composition, labeling, and performance[15]. Products that fail are publicly listed on SAMR's website with the seller name, platform, and specific violation — causing immediate reputational and commercial damage.

Escalating Penalty Structure

Enforcement follows a graduated escalation model:

  • First offense (minor): Warning + corrective order + public disclosure
  • Repeat or serious: Fines (RMB 50,000–2 million), product confiscation, business suspension
  • Criminal threshold: If substandard goods cause bodily injury or death, or if the value exceeds RMB 50,000, the case is referred to the Public Security Bureau for criminal prosecution under Article 140 of the Criminal Law (Production and Sale of Fake or Substandard Goods)[16]

Platform-Driven Enforcement

Increasingly, platforms themselves act as enforcement agents to avoid their own Article 38 liability. JD.com, Alibaba, and Pinduoduo now operate dedicated quality assurance teams that conduct random sampling of seller inventory — at the seller's expense. A seller with a 2%+ complaint rate on product quality typically faces delisting, deposit forfeiture, and blacklisting across the platform group[17].

What Foreign Brands Should Do

Based on the regulatory framework and enforcement patterns above, here is a practical compliance checklist for foreign brands selling on Chinese e-commerce platforms:

  1. Document every link in the supply chain. Maintain product quality certificates, factory inspection reports, and customs clearance documents for every batch. Digitize and archive with a minimum 3-year retention period — SAMR audits can reach back that far.
  2. Invest in Chinese-language labeling compliance. This is the enforcement action most commonly triggered against foreign brands. Work with a local labeling specialist to ensure every SKU meets GB standard marking requirements before listing.
  3. Verify platform algorithm filings. If the platform uses AI for quality authentication, confirm that the algorithm is filed with CAC. Request filing confirmation as part of your platform onboarding due diligence.
  4. Appoint a domestic service agent. For CBEC channels, ensure a registered Chinese entity is contractually obligated to handle returns, refunds, recalls, and regulatory inquiries.
  5. Monitor SAMR spot-check results. SAMR publishes all e-commerce spot-check failures quarterly. Set up a monitoring process to catch any mention of your brand or SKU within 48 hours of publication — the reputational window is short.
  6. Build a consumer complaint response system. Under Article 38, platforms are incentivized to act on consumer complaints quickly. Ensure you have a China-based team or agent that can respond to quality complaints within 24 hours to prevent escalation to platform-level enforcement.
