How to Analyze Any China Regulatory Policy in 4 Layers

A framework that starts with why — demonstrated through China's AI regulation
June 3, 2026

Most China policy analysis answers one question: what does the regulation say? To make decisions about China, you need to answer four questions in order.

The 4-Layer Framework

Layer 0 — The Logic: Why does this regulation exist?

Understanding the purpose helps predict the next regulation before it arrives.

Layer 1 — The Text: What does it say?

The written rules. Most analysis stops here.

Layer 2 — Implementation: How are rules applied in practice?

Written rules and practical implementation may differ.

Layer 3 — The Opportunity: What can you do?

Within the framework, where are the viable paths forward?

Layer 0: The Logic Behind China's AI Regulation

China's AI regulatory approach reflects several consistent principles that shape technology policy. Understanding these principles helps businesses anticipate regulatory direction.

Principle 1: Security and Stability Alongside Innovation

China's regulatory framework is designed to ensure technological development proceeds in a controlled and orderly manner. Each AI regulation since 2022 has addressed real-world concerns including deepfake cases and AI-generated misinformation. The approach reflects a considered balance between enabling innovation and managing societal impact. This explains why China has established a comprehensive AI regulatory framework focused on responsible development.

Principle 2: Data Jurisdiction Is Clearly Defined

The Data Security Law (2021) establishes that data generated within China's jurisdiction is subject to Chinese law. This principle has remained consistent across all subsequent regulations. For foreign AI companies, this means data handling must comply with Chinese legal requirements including data localization provisions where applicable.

Principle 3: Consistent Standards for All Market Participants

China's regulatory framework applies to both domestic and foreign companies. Domestic AI companies have built compliance systems incrementally as regulations developed over time. Foreign companies entering the market must meet the same standards. China welcomes foreign technology that brings unique value, particularly in advanced manufacturing, specialized medical technology, and enterprise software.

Principle 4: Regulation Serves Multiple Objectives

China's regulatory framework addresses consumer protection, security, and industrial development goals simultaneously. AI regulations establish compliance standards, create a professional services ecosystem, and encourage technology cooperation between domestic and international companies.

Value for decision-makers: Understanding these principles helps anticipate regulatory direction. When a new technology emerges, these same principles can inform expectations about how it will be regulated.

Layer 1: The Regulatory Framework

China's AI regulatory framework has been developed through four key actions since 2022:

Algorithm Filing (March 2022)

AI systems with recommendation or ranking functions must register with the Cyberspace Administration of China. Over 6,800 algorithms registered as of mid-2026.

Deep Synthesis Rules (January 2023)

AI-generated content must carry visible labels. Requirements apply to services accessible in China regardless of company origin.

Generative AI Measures (August 2023)

Services require safety assessment before launch, lawful training data, and content compliance measures.

AI Law Draft (Expected 2026-2027)

Proposes risk classification tiers, a regulatory sandbox, and formalized cross-border data rules for AI training data.

Sources: CAC regulations and announcements, 2022-2025. All publicly available.

Layer 2: Implementation Considerations

Note on foreign licensing: As of mid-2026, publicly available information indicates no independent foreign-owned generative AI service has been confirmed to have received operating approval in China. This reflects compliance requirements and regulatory procedures rather than any explicit restriction in the regulations themselves.
Note on data localization: The implementation of data localization provisions has resulted in most foreign companies maintaining AI training data within China. Cross-border transfer requires a security assessment with comprehensive documentation requirements.
Note on algorithm disclosure: The information required for algorithm filing has led some companies with proprietary models to evaluate their approach carefully. This is a practical consideration rather than a regulatory barrier.
Key consideration: Planning based only on the written regulations may not capture the full scope of compliance requirements in practice.

Layer 3: Practical Paths Forward

Path A: Enterprise/B2B Market

Selling AI tools to Chinese enterprises faces lighter regulatory requirements compared to consumer-facing AI. No safety assessment is needed for B2B tools without public-facing content generation.

Path B: Local Partnership

Partnering with a Chinese company that has regulatory clearance is the most common operating model. Under this arrangement, the partner handles compliance while the foreign company provides technology.

Path C: Preparation and Monitoring

Building partner relationships and compliance infrastructure in preparation for regulatory developments, while monitoring the AI Law sandbox provisions as a potential entry signal.

Note: The appropriate path depends on the specific product type, target customers, and corporate circumstances. A medical AI company faces different considerations than a marketing AI company under the same regulatory framework.
