How China's AI Regulation Actually Works

A practical compliance guide for foreign investors and tech companies — what changed, what it costs, and what comes next
June 3, 2026 · Reading time: 12 min
TL;DR — China's AI regulation is not a single law but a four-layer framework built since 2022. Foreign companies face three hard barriers: no independent operating license has been granted, data localization requirements are strict, and algorithm disclosure creates IP risks. Estimated compliance cost for a mid-size entrant: $200,000-1M upfront plus ongoing staffing. The AI Law (expected 2026-2027) will add a formal risk classification system. Companies that want access to China's AI market need a local partner strategy — independent entry is not currently viable.

1. The Framework: Not One Law, Four Layers

Most coverage treats China's AI regulation as a single event (the August 2023 Generative AI Measures). This is misleading. The current framework is the result of four distinct regulatory actions layered over four years:

LayerEffectiveScopeKey Requirement
1. Algorithm RegulationMar 2022Recommendation algorithmsFile algorithm basics with CAC
2. Deep Synthesis RulesJan 2023AI-generated content (video, audio, text)Watermark all AI content
3. Generative AI MeasuresAug 2023All generative AI servicesSafety assessment + data compliance
4. AI Law (Draft)Expected 2026-2027All high-risk AI applicationsRisk classification + sandbox system

Sources: (1) Internet Information Service Algorithmic Recommendation管理规定 (CAC, 2022); (2) Deep Synthesis管理规定 (CAC, 2023); (3) Generative AI Interim Measures (CAC, 2023); (4) AI Law Draft (NPC, published for public comment 2025).

2. The Real Barrier: No Foreign AI License Has Been Granted

This is the single most important fact for any foreign investor: as of June 2026, no independent foreign-owned generative AI service has received an operating license in China. This is not stated explicitly in any regulation — it is the cumulative effect of the framework.

Why the License Is Effectively Blocked

Three requirements collectively prevent foreign entry:

  • Data localization: The Data Security Law (2021) requires critical data to be stored and processed in China. For AI training pipelines that include Chinese user data, this means building a complete local infrastructure — servers, content review teams, compliance staff. Estimated setup cost: $500,000-2M minimum.
  • Algorithm filing: The CAC requires filers to describe their algorithmic logic and training data sources. For proprietary foreign models, this effectively requires disclosing elements of the algorithm's decision-making framework — a significant IP concern.
  • Safety assessment: The assessment process evaluates whether AI outputs comply with Chinese content standards, which differ from Western standards on political topics, historical narratives, and sensitive events. No published criteria exist for passing — creating regulatory uncertainty.
Key insight: The licensing barrier is not a written ban — it's a de facto wall created by the cumulative cost and uncertainty of compliance. No rational foreign company would invest $1M+ without guaranteed licensing, and no guaranteed licensing path has been established.

What Foreign Companies Actually Do

Based on publicly reported arrangements, three operating models have emerged:

  • API-only (offshore): OpenAI, Anthropic — their services are accessible from China but not officially approved. Users access via VPN. Revenue is generated overseas, not from within China.
  • Local partner model: Microsoft's Copilot for M365 operates through a local entity, with content filtering applied to comply with Chinese standards. This model adds 30-50% to operating costs (analyst estimates).
  • Technology licensing: Some Western AI companies have explored licensing their models to Chinese partners, but no major deal has been publicly confirmed as complete as of mid-2026.

Sources: Company public disclosures, press reports on OpenAI/Anthropic/Microsoft China operations, CAC public communications. Operating cost estimates are analyst projections, not company-reported figures.

4. What Changes When the AI Law Passes

The AI Law (Draft), expected to pass in 2026-2027, will introduce three structural changes worth tracking:

Risk Classification System

The draft law proposes four tiers: minimal, limited, high, and unacceptable risk. Unacceptable risk AI applications (e.g., social credit scoring using AI, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces) would be prohibited. High-risk applications (e.g., AI in hiring, credit evaluation, education) would face the heaviest compliance requirements — including mandatory third-party audits and human oversight mechanisms.

Relevance to foreign investors: If your AI product falls into "high-risk," expect 6-12 months of compliance review before launch. If "limited risk," the process is faster but still requires registration.

Government Sandbox

The draft includes a regulatory sandbox provision — allowing approved companies to test AI applications with relaxed compliance requirements in controlled environments. This could be the most viable entry path for foreign companies, but no sandbox has been established yet and participation criteria are unclear.

Cross-Border Data Rules

The AI Law will likely codify data localization requirements specifically for AI training data. This is the single most impactful provision for foreign companies — and also the most uncertain, as it remains under negotiation in the legislative process.

My assessment: The AI Law will not suddenly open the market for foreign companies. It will formalize existing regulatory practices, reducing some uncertainty while creating new compliance obligations. The sandbox provision is the most important signal — if foreign companies are invited to participate, it would mark a genuine opening. If the sandbox is restricted to domestic companies only, the current de facto barrier remains.

Sources: AI Law Draft (NPC public comment version, 2025); NPC legislative agenda announcements; CAC public briefings. Sandbox provisions and risk classification tiers based on draft text — subject to change before final passage.

5. What I Recommend: A Practical Path Forward

For foreign companies and investors asking "can we enter China's AI market," the honest answer is: not independently, not yet. But there are realistic engagement paths:

  • For AI product companies — Partner with a Chinese company that already has regulatory clearance. The partner handles compliance; you provide technology. Revenue share models are the standard arrangement. Expect to give up 30-50% of China revenue to the partner.
  • For investors — Monitor two triggers: (1) the AI Law's sandbox provisions when finalized, (2) any CAC announcement of a foreign company receiving a license. Either event would signal a policy shift. Until then, the de facto barrier holds.
  • For enterprise AI suppliers — Selling AI infrastructure/tools to Chinese companies (rather than operating consumer-facing AI services) faces lighter regulation. The Algorithm Filing requirement still applies, but no safety assessment is needed for B2B tools that don't generate public content.
Bottom line: China's AI market is not closed by law — it is closed by the cumulative cost and uncertainty of compliance. The companies that succeed will be those that treat regulatory navigation as a core capability, not a legal afterthought. The sandbox provisions of the upcoming AI Law are the single most important development to watch.
