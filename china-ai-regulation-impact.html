How to Analyze Any China Regulatory Policy in 3 Layers

A reusable framework for foreign investors — demonstrated through China's AI regulation
June 3, 2026 · Series: China AI Regulation (Part 1 of 3)

Why this framework exists

Most China policy analysis tells you what the regulation says. That's not enough. You need to know: what does this mean for my business, and what should I do about it?

This article introduces a three-layer framework you can apply to any China regulatory policy — then demonstrates it using AI regulation as a case study.

The 3-Layer Framework

Layer 1 — The Text: What does the regulation actually say? (Most analysis stops here. Don't.)

Layer 2 — The Gap: How is it actually enforced? Is there a difference between what's written and what happens?

Layer 3 — The Opportunity: Where is the opening for foreign companies within the regulatory framework?

After these three layers, you should be able to answer: should I enter, wait, or stay out?

Layer 1: The Text — What China's AI Regulation Actually Says

China's AI regulatory framework has been built through four sequential actions since 2022. Each targets a different aspect of AI:

Step 1: Algorithm Filing (March 2022)

Any AI system that recommends content or makes decisions for users must file basic algorithmic information with the Cyberspace Administration of China. Over 6,800 algorithms registered as of mid-2026.

🎯 Question this layer answers: Is your product covered? → If your AI has any recommendation/ranking function, yes.

Step 2: Deep Synthesis Rules (January 2023)

AI-generated content (text, image, audio, video) must carry visible labels. Applies regardless of where the company is based — if the service is accessible from China, it must comply.

🎯 Question this layer answers: What operational changes are needed? → Add watermarking and content tracking.

Step 3: Generative AI Measures (August 2023)

Services must pass a safety assessment before public launch, use only lawfully obtained training data, and ensure outputs comply with content standards. Assessment takes 2-4 months.

🎯 Question this layer answers: Can I get approval? → Unknown — no foreign company has yet.

Step 4: AI Law (Draft, expected 2026-2027)

Introduces risk classification tiers (minimal, limited, high, unacceptable), a regulatory sandbox, and formalized cross-border data rules for AI training data.

🎯 Question this layer answers: Will the rules change? → Yes, but likely not in favor of foreign entry.

Sources: (1) CAC Algorithm Regulation 2022; (2) Deep Synthesis Rules 2023; (3) Generative AI Interim Measures 2023; (4) AI Law Draft 2025. All publicly available.

Layer 1 takeaway for foreign companies: The written rules apply equally to domestic and foreign companies. No explicit ban exists. But Layer 1 is never the full story in China.

Layer 2: The Gap — How It Actually Works

The written rules and the actual enforcement landscape differ in three critical ways. This gap is where most foreign companies make mistakes.

Gap #1: "No foreign license has been granted" is not in any regulation — but it's the reality.
The CAC has not published a list of licensed foreign AI services, nor has it stated that foreign companies are excluded. But as of mid-2026, no independent foreign-owned generative AI service has received operating approval in China. This is not a policy — it's the combined effect of high compliance cost, regulatory uncertainty, and the absence of a clear approval pathway.
Gap #2: Data localization is enforced more strictly than the written rules suggest.
The Data Security Law requires "critical data" to stay in China. In practice, the CAC has interpreted this broadly for AI training data. Cross-border transfer of AI-related data faces an estimated 50%+ rejection rate (industry estimates — official statistics are not published). The practical effect: most foreign AI companies keep all China data in China, even when the written rules might allow transfer.
Gap #3: Algorithm disclosure requirements create a de facto IP barrier.
The written rules ask for "basic algorithmic logic." In practice, the level of detail required has deterred companies with proprietary AI models from filing. This is not a written barrier — it's a practical one.
Layer 2 takeaway: The gap between written rules and enforcement is where the real barriers are. Foreign companies that plan based only on Layer 1 will underestimate the cost and complexity of entry.

Sources: Industry reports, company disclosures, CAC public communications. Rejection rates are industry estimates — no official data published.

Layer 3: The Opportunity — What You Can Actually Do

Layer 3 identifies the openings that exist within the regulatory framework. For China's AI regulation, three viable paths emerge:

Path A: Enterprise/B2B Market (Most Viable Today)

Selling AI infrastructure or tools to Chinese enterprises faces lighter regulation than consumer-facing AI. The Algorithm Filing still applies, but no safety assessment is needed for B2B tools without public-facing content generation. Estimated entry cost: $50,000-150,000 for legal setup + 6-12 months for compliance clearance.

Path B: Local Partner Model (Most Common)

Partner with a Chinese company that already has regulatory clearance. You provide technology, they handle compliance and customer-facing operations. Standard revenue share: 50-70% to the foreign partner. This is the model Microsoft uses for Copilot in China.

Path C: Monitor + Prepare (Lowest Risk)

Track the AI Law sandbox provisions and any CAC announcement of a foreign license. Use the time to build partner relationships and prepare compliance infrastructure. If the sandbox opens to foreign companies, those who prepared early will move fastest.

Layer 3 takeaway: The question is not "can I enter China's AI market." It's "which path matches my risk tolerance and timeline." Each path has different requirements, costs, and timeframes. The right answer depends on your specific product, target customers, and corporate risk appetite.

Applying This Framework to Any China Regulation

This same three-layer approach works for any regulated industry in China — pharmaceuticals, finance, automotive, data, energy. Each time, the questions are the same:

Layer 1 (The Text): What does the regulation say? Is there an explicit restriction on foreign participation?

Layer 2 (The Gap): How is it enforced in practice? What's the difference between written rules and actual barriers?

Layer 3 (The Opportunity): Where are the openings? What paths exist that others have missed?

The real value of this framework: It prevents the two most common mistakes foreign companies make: (1) assuming written rules reflect reality, and (2) giving up at Layer 1 without searching for Layer 3 opportunities.

Every industry has different gaps and opportunities.
